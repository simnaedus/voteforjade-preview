Lessons From 2020–2025

What won, what lost, and why it matters for Feb 2027

Threat

February specials are tiny electorates

The 2021 mayor's race was decided on Feb 23, 2021 with just 10,670 votes — Biss won it outright with 7,786. Jade's special is on the same calendar date in 2027. Whoever owns the ground game owns a February election; ~8,000–11,000 votes likely wins it.

Source: Certified 2021 results · Election-night report

Opportunity

The turnout gap IS the strategy

2025 turnout: Ward 6 47.5% vs Ward 5 18.7% and Ward 8 24.3%. Wards 2/5/8 — the nonwhite-majority wards Jade's platform speaks to directly — undervote by 20+ points. Closing even half that gap in a low-turnout special is decisive.

Source: Clerk precinct table (CSNA PDF) · Turnout analysis

Opportunity

2021: challengers CAN beat institutions

The 2021 cycle was an anti-incumbent wave: three sitting council members lost (W1 by 26 votes, W4 and W8 incumbents ousted at the primary). An outsider-with-a-cause message has recent proof of concept here.

Source: Certified W1 result · Four new aldermen

Threat

Ward 7: high-turnout opposition

The only ward Biss lost in 2025 (48.2/51.8, Ryan Field backlash) — >80% white, >60% of households $120k+, and it turns out at 42–45%. Development framing that reads as "imposed on neighborhoods" activates this ward against you. Jade's "balance, not division" zoning message needs to be airtight here.

Source: Ward results (CSNA PDF) · Ward profile

Opportunity

Evanston votes for structural change

Ranked-choice voting passed with ~82% in Nov 2022 — a 4:1 landslide. The electorate has real appetite for reform and doing things differently; a first-time candidate is not a liability here if framed as renewal.

Source: RoundTable · Ballotpedia · 2024 court ruling

Watch

Margins here are razor thin

W1 council: 26 votes (2021), 56 votes (2025). W8 in 2021: 32 votes. In ward-level races — and a special will behave like one citywide — every block walked matters. Field > ads in Evanston municipals.

Source: W1 2021 certified · 2021 margins analysis

Watch

Mail ballots leak

April 2025: 10,038 mail ballots issued but only 4,324 arrived by election day. A ballot-chase program (call/text every VBM requester until it's returned) is close to free votes.

Source: RoundTable final-data analysis

Opportunity

2023 specials: ~10k voters decide

April 2023 special elections drew 17–23% turnout; W2 was won with 696 votes. Specials reward organization, not name ID. A disciplined 90-day field plan beats an establishment endorsement list in this format.

Source: W2 result · W9 result · Turnout

Threat

Uncontested ≠ engaged

W9 council was uncontested in 2021 AND 2025; W4 faced only write-ins in 2025. Large pockets of Evanston haven't seen a real campaign in years — they need reactivation, not persuasion, but they also have no voting habit for city races.

Source: 2025 ballot guide · No primaries 2025

The plan in 60 seconds

“Evanston is a vibrant and inclusive community that celebrates its arts, culture, history, and urban landscape… We acknowledge our past and strive for a better future.” — Vision statement, p. 48

Six citywide goals (pp. 49–51): healthy community · local economy · climate action · transportation options · housing stability, access and variety · arts and culture. Land use runs on a centers-and-corridors framework (p. 51) with a Future Land Use Map guiding the zoning rewrite. The plan's High growth scenario (+32,147 people by 2045) explicitly assumes 'more significant changes to local development regulations' (p. 47).

Everything above cites the adopted plan: EVANSTON Comp Plan_12.19.25.pdf. Full chapter digests with page-cited goals/policies: dashboard-data/ee2045_digest_*.md

Honest-broker flags (gaps we verified in the adopted text): No implementation matrix — Ch. 15 defers everything to a post-adoption 'Year 1 action plan' (pp. 182–183). No numeric housing unit targets or AMI set-asides beyond definitions. No sequencing of single-family changes in adopted policy (press-reported sequencing lives outside the plan text). Reparations absent from adopted policy chapters (Appendix A engagement only). 'Missing middle' never appears as a phrase. Hedge ledger: vacancy tax, deconversion ban, and NU collaboration are all 'explore'; neighborhood preference is 'consider' (H 1.10). ECON 10.5 ('reduce vagrancy and panhandling') sits in tension with Ch. 11's health-first framing — a debate-prep item.
What Evanston Is Saying

Sentiment by flashpoint

HIGH HEAT

Single-family zoning / 'four units on every lot' (missing middle density)

They like
  • Comp plan goals direct the city to allow greater housing density in all neighborhoods, including ending single-family-only restrictions - seen by advocates as the core equity win (Evanston RoundTable reporting; pro-plan council bloc — 2026-01-25 · source)
  • Legalizing multi-unit housing framed as a path to homeownership for Black and middle-income residents priced out of Evanston (Rezoning for a Better Evanston (Roger Williams, realtor; Frank Hill, 7th Ward resident) — 2025-09-22 · source)
They don't like
  • Draft zoning allowed 35-ft, three-story buildings of up to 4 units on all lots currently zoned single-family or two-flat; CSNA called it a dramatic upzone of 'almost every Evanston neighborhood' (Central Street Neighbors Association — 2024-2026 (ongoing page) · source)
  • At the joint 6th/7th Ward meeting (130+ attendees), the large majority appeared opposed and at times suspicious of the project's goals; R1 standards would shrink minimum lot size 7,200 to 6,500 sq ft and raise lot coverage 30% to 35% (North Evanston residents (6th/7th Wards) — 2024-12-16 · source)
They fear
  • By-right development means loss of neighborhood control; opponents repeated this at hearings (housing advocates dispute the claim) “you cannot control the level of density if building is by right” (Former council candidate Meg Welch, as quoted critically by a YIMBW-leaning writer — 2025 · source)
  • Fear that upzoning triggers teardowns of modest homes and accelerates displacement rather than affordability “this product is going to accelerate displacement” (Ald. Clare Kelly (1st), explaining her no vote on the comp plan — 2026-01-26 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Council amendments (fall 2025) directed that zoning changes in single-family districts come AFTER corridor/downtown areas are considered - reported by press as settled sequencing. CAVEAT (verified by full-text search of the adopted 12/19/25 plan): no sequencing language appears in the adopted document itself; the commitment only becomes binding when written into the zoning code - which the next mayor oversees. (City Council (amendment process, Oct 2025 - Jan 2026) — 2026-01 · source)
  • Rezoning for a Better Evanston argues 4 units is not enough - duplexes/triplexes remain unaffordable to middle-income buyers; wants 6-7 unit condo buildings designed to match historic aesthetics (Rezoning for a Better Evanston — 2025-09 · source)
Easy win
  • Publicly commit to the press-reported sequencing (corridors/downtown first, single-family districts last, with data checkpoints) AND commit to writing it into the zoning code, since it is not in the adopted plan text - reassures skeptics while strengthening the 'vision into law' message. (source)
  • Champion the Black middle-income homeownership framing (condo ownership in small multiplexes, anti-redlining repair) - it cross-cuts the NIMBY/YIMBY divide and has a realtor-founded local org behind it (source)
HIGH HEAT

Process, speed and trust (timeline, consultant, Halik removal, HUD transparency)

They like
  • Council responded to backlash: 8-0 vote in Jan 2025 decoupled the comp plan from the zoning code and delayed the zoning vote until after the April 2025 election - widely credited as the process correction (City Council — 2025-01-13 · source)
  • For round two, council required the zoning RFP itself to come back for approval before bidding, and the new schedule is deliberately slow: engagement in 2027, iterative drafting 2027-28, adoption end of 2028 (City Council / city staff — 2026-02-06 · source)
They don't like
  • Original plan to rewrite comp plan AND zoning code simultaneously in about a year drew far-reaching criticism as rushed; 85 public commenters at the Jan 2025 meeting, most on Envision (Residents at Jan 13, 2025 council meeting — 2025-01-14 · source)
  • Frustration with original consultant HDR Inc. was bipartisan on council; the draft code it produced was effectively shelved and the city started over with a new RFP (City Council (both blocs) — 2026-02-23 · source)
They fear
  • Opposition coalition alleges the plan was shaped around a federally aligned housing timeline and HUD grant commitments made without full public disclosure or council authorization “did not emerge from a transparent, resident-led planning process” (Evanston Action Coalition (cross-posted by CSNA) — 2026-01-26 · source)
  • Biss's decision not to reappoint LUC Commissioner George Halik mid-review was read as punishing a critic; a 527-signature petition compared it to 'removing a juror mid-trial' (Jenny Washburn, petition author) “removing a juror mid-trial” (Petition signers / residents at council public comment — 2025-04-16 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Letter writers on both sides asked for the zoning process to reconcile public debate with the city's pursuit of federal resources - i.e., disclose the commitments and align the timeline openly (RoundTable letter — 2026-02-21 · source)
  • Pro-plan voices also asked for repair: 'Trust and dialogue critical for Envision Evanston' letter urged rebuilding relationships before the zoning phase (RoundTable letter — 2025-08-01 · source)
Easy win
  • Promise a transparency package: publish all federal/HUD commitments, an amendment-tracking dashboard for the zoning rewrite, and ward-by-ward engagement sessions run before drafting. Costs nothing, defuses the #1 grievance, and distances the candidate from Biss-era process complaints without criticizing the plan itself (source)
  • Commit to respecting commission independence (no mid-process removals) - directly answers the Halik episode, which angered even some plan supporters (source)
HIGH HEAT

Building heights, downtown and corridor development

They like
  • Consolidating 41 zoning districts to ~17 and enabling mixed-use 'centers and corridors' was pitched as simplification and transit-oriented growth (City staff / Envision framework — 2024-12-04 · source)
  • City's own Housing4All survey responses included support for transit-oriented residential development (City of Evanston survey respondents (940+ open-ended responses) — 2026-01 · source)
They don't like
  • Draft M3 district would allow 100-ft buildings by right along Chicago Avenue where current caps are 45 ft; draft also removed downtown height limits and converted business districts to 65-100 ft corridors (RoundTable analysis of draft code; CSNA — 2025-01-23 · source)
  • Downtown Evanston Residents Association formed (with Ald. Clare Kelly's support) explicitly to oppose new high-rise developments and influence zoning over the next several years; aiming for 500 members and a zoning committee (Downtown Evanston Residents Association (DERA) — 2026-06-03 · source)
They fear
  • By-right tall buildings next to low-rise blocks; canyon effect on corridors; developers benefiting over residents (a recurring public-comment theme) (Corridor and downtown residents; LUC public hearing commenters — 2024-11 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • LUC spent early 2025 looking for 'common ground' on heights - stepping heights down near residential edges, keeping some special-use review; expect the new consultant round to revisit corridor caps (Land Use Commission — 2025-01 · source)
Easy win
  • Support height step-downs and design standards at residential edges while defending corridor growth - lets the candidate be pro-growth on Chicago Ave/downtown but 'protective' where towers meet houses. Engage DERA early; they are new, organizing fast, and want to be consulted (source)
HIGH HEAT

Affordability, displacement and racial equity (does upzoning deliver?)

They like
  • Housing advocates argue exclusionary zoning preserves segregation; Equitable Zoning Project recommends multi-family housing in all residential zones and removing 'character' as a decision standard (Joining Forces for Affordable Housing / Connections for the Homeless — 2020-2026 (campaign ongoing) · source)
  • Dozens of Connections workers and advocates rallied before the Jan 2025 LUC hearing; organizer Rodney Orr acknowledged 'change is scary' but said rising rents and taxes without intervention 'is even scarier' “change is scary” (Connections for the Homeless rally — 2025-01-08 · source)
They don't like
  • Critics argue market-rate upzoning is not an affordability policy: 'Envision Evanston isn't about affordable housing' (RoundTable letter writer — 2024-12-17 · source)
  • Opposition letter called the plan 'a blueprint for inequality' and characterized the agenda as government-driven gentrification benefiting developers (RoundTable letter writer — 2025-01-13 · source)
They fear
  • Teardown-driven displacement of legacy (especially Black 5th Ward) homeowners and renters; Kelly's no-vote rationale was displacement acceleration (Ald. Clare Kelly; letter writers on historical injustice and displacement — 2025-07-30 · source)
  • Even the lead pro-housing nonprofit warned upzoning alone is insufficient: Connections CEO Betty Bogg said the zoning changes need a stronger Inclusionary Housing Ordinance, while dismissing blanket opposition “this is what NIMBYism looks like” (Betty Bogg, CEO, Connections for the Homeless — 2025-01-08 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Housing4All as adopted couples density with preservation: preserve up to 1,000 affordable units and create 2,500-4,000 new homes by 2036; council accepted 25 of 64 amendments (City Council — 2026-05-11 · source)
  • Strengthen the Inclusionary Housing Ordinance alongside the zoning rewrite - the ask that unites Connections, Joining Forces, and some skeptics (Connections for the Homeless / Joining Forces — 2025-01 · source)
Easy win
  • Pair every pro-density message with a concrete anti-displacement tool (IHO strengthening, legacy-homeowner property tax relief advocacy, NOAH preservation fund from Housing4All). Answers the strongest anti-plan attack ('gentrification blueprint') with policy, not rhetoric (source)
MEDIUM HEAT

Parking minimums and transit

They like
  • Advocates and Biss backed eliminating parking minimums as a cost-cutter for housing; climate-conscious housing groups urged it (Mayor Biss; housing/climate advocates — 2024 · source)
  • Issue largely settled from above: state law now bans parking minimums near transit (covering nearly all of Evanston), and the city decided in May 2026 to implement the ban without local changes (City of Evanston — 2026-05-10 · source)
They don't like
  • Neighborhood groups fear on-street parking spillover and congestion when new multi-unit buildings come without off-street parking (recurring ward-meeting theme in north Evanston) (North Evanston residents; CSNA — 2024-12 · source)
They fear
  • Street parking scarcity near two-flat/fourplex conversions, especially blocks far from rail (Ward meeting commenters — 2024-12 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Residential permit-parking management and transportation demand tools as the compromise position (city retains these levers even under state preemption) (2026-05 · source)
Easy win
  • Blame-shift gracefully: parking minimums are now largely a state matter (Pritzker-era law), so the candidate can support the policy while pointing skeptics to permit-parking protections - low-cost issue to be reasonable on (source)
MEDIUM HEAT

Historic preservation and neighborhood character

They like
  • City preservation planner reassured residents the Preservation Ordinance takes precedence over new zoning “the zoning yields to the Preservation Ordinance” (Evanston Preservation Planner (at Feb 2025 joint ward meeting) — 2025-02-19 · source)
They don't like
  • Residents feared new development goals would allow 'unrestricted demolition and rebuilding'; roughly 430 individual landmarks outside historic districts are seen as vulnerable (Joint ward meeting attendees; Preservation Commission — 2025-02-20 · source)
  • From the pro-housing side, residential landmarking is attacked as a veto tool: letter urged fixing Envision 'by removing residential landmarking' (RoundTable letter writer (pro-housing) — 2025-06-23 · source)
They fear
  • Loss of 'Evanston's character' - a recurring letters theme ('What happened to Evanston's character?'); Joining Forces conversely wants 'character' deleted from code as subjective and exclusionary (Letter writers vs. Joining Forces — 2025-02-19 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Preservation Commission's open letter and recommendations: demolition-delay tools, pocket historic districts, Neighborhood Conservation Districts to give residents stakeholder roles (Evanston Preservation Commission — 2025-07-28 · source)
Easy win
  • Endorse the Preservation Commission's mitigation toolkit (demolition delay, conservation districts) and repeat 'zoning yields to preservation' - a concrete, already-vetted reassurance that costs the housing agenda little (source)
MEDIUM HEAT

Property taxes, infrastructure and city data credibility

They like
  • Growth advocates argue a larger tax base spreads the levy and supports services; the city's Housing4All survey logged support for rental assistance and housing-related climate action alongside tax concerns (City survey respondents — 2026-01 · source)
They don't like
  • Skeptics attacked the plan's demographic premises - is Evanston even growing? CSNA published analyses disputing growth projections, and a RoundTable letter charged 'Evanston literally doesn't know what it's doing' on data (CSNA; RoundTable letter writer — 2025-02-23 · source)
They fear
  • Property tax increases (top-of-list in the city's own 940+ open-ended survey responses) and infrastructure/school capacity strain from added density (City survey respondents; ward meeting commenters — 2026-01 · source)
Wants tweaking
  • Demands for infrastructure capacity studies and honest population baselines before zoning changes reach residential districts (CSNA; north-ward residents — 2025-2026 · source)
Easy win
  • Commit to publishing an independent infrastructure-and-fiscal-impact analysis as phase one of the zoning rewrite (the 2026-27 planning phase is happening anyway) - converts a critique into a deliverable (source)
MEDIUM HEAT

State preemption politics (Pritzker BUILD Act) and outside momentum

They like
  • Statewide bills would enact some Envision-style reforms regardless of local outcome; YIMBY Illinois sees a 'perfect storm' of grassroots and national momentum - gives pro-plan actors leverage and cover (YIMBY Illinois (Ben Wolfenstein) — 2026-04-01 · source)
They don't like
  • Residents and some advocates divided on Springfield overriding local control while Evanston pursues its own code (Residents quoted by Daily Northwestern — 2026-04-21 · source)
They fear
  • Local-control loss: if Evanston stalls, the state decides anyway (motivates both camps differently) (Both camps — 2026 · source)
Easy win
  • Frame the local rewrite as 'Evanston writing its own rules before Springfield writes them for us' - converts preemption anxiety into an argument FOR finishing the zoning rewrite locally and thoughtfully (source)
Jade's Ground

Plank ↔ plan ↔ message

Turn the vision into law (zoning rewrite with legal precision)

What the plan says: The plan explicitly defers implementation: Ch. 15 (pp. 182–183) contains NO action matrix — only a 'Year 1 action plan' to be written post-adoption, annual reports, and 5-year reviews. The zoning rewrite restarted in 2026 after the first consultant draft was shelved; code adoption is targeted for end of 2028 — squarely in the next mayor's term.

Public sentiment: Process distrust is a top-2 flashpoint; even supporters criticize the shelved HDR draft and rushed timeline.

Recommended message: This is Jade's core case: the plan is adopted but unexecuted. The Year 1 action plan and the zoning code are the next mayor's to write — 'the vision exists; it needs a lawyer to make it law.'

Sources: Plan Ch.15, pp.182–183 · Zoning scope of work (RoundTable) · Adoption 5–4 (RoundTable)

Balance, not division (single-family + other housing types side by side)

What the plan says: H 2.1 (p. 130) commits to by-right small-scale multi-unit housing 'in all residential districts,' subject to lot standards; H 2.2 smaller lots; H 2.8–2.9 pre-approved ADU/duplex/triplex plans. Counterweight: H 1.10 (p. 130) says the City will 'consider the preferences of residents in each neighborhood' — the plan's own balance clause.

Public sentiment: Highest-heat theme. '4 units on every lot' is the attack line; 130+ residents turned out against it in north-ward meetings. Advocates call it the core equity win.

Recommended message: Quote H 2.1 AND H 1.10 together: density with neighborhood voice is IN the plan. Note press reports say single-family changes were sequenced last — but that sequencing is NOT in the adopted policy text; it lives in council direction on the zoning phase. Jade can own pinning it down in code.

Sources: H 2.1 / H 1.10, p.130 · Adoption preview (RoundTable)

Evanston Family Promise (schools + housing are one crisis)

What the plan says: H 1.11 (p. 130): collaborate with D65 to assess young families' housing needs and 'develop strategies to attract and retain them.' H 1.4: more family-sized units with multiple bedrooms. Plan cites D65's deficit and enrollment decline in its own data chapters.

Public sentiment: Affordability-vs-displacement is a high-heat theme; families and D65 finances recur across coverage.

Recommended message: Jade's Family Promise is essentially H 1.11 + H 1.4 with a name and an owner. 'It's in the plan — I'll fund it and run it.'

Sources: H 1.11 / H 1.4, p.130

Hold Northwestern accountable

What the plan says: The plan is soft here: CS 6.1 is a single sentence; H 1.8 and H 2.12 are 'assess/explore' collaborations on student housing; the NU bed-deficit passage (~14,900, p. 127) carries a footnote that council approved its language. Appendix A engagement records include the idea that NU 'could be a financial partner in housing solutions.'

Public sentiment: NU accountability polls well across factions; the plan's hedged language is a visible gap.

Recommended message: Easy contrast: 'The plan says explore. As an attorney, I negotiate and enforce.' Cite the bed deficit from the plan's own text.

Sources: pp.83, 127, 130–131 · Jade's filing coverage (Daily NU)

Listen first (real engagement before decisions)

What the plan says: Ch. 2 documents the city's engagement and its gaps — survey participants skewed unrepresentative vs. city demographics (city's own tables). The plan promises 'a culture of active community participation' (Goal, p. 49) and annual public implementation reports (p. 182).

Public sentiment: Process/trust is a top-2 flashpoint: Halik removal, '527-signature petition,' undisclosed HUD commitments, consultant failure — grievances that unite both sides.

Recommended message: Own the process fix without trashing the plan: transparency package, ward-first engagement on the zoning phase, publish everything. Inherits none of the baggage; answers all of it.

Sources: Ch.2; p.49; p.182 · Zoning scope feedback process

Defend the reparations commitment

What the plan says: Notable and flaggable: reparations / Restorative Housing appear NOWHERE in the adopted policy chapters — only in Appendix A engagement records. The plan documents the Black population decline (16,749 in 1990 → 12,542 in 2020, p. 272) but adopts no reparations policy.

Public sentiment: Displacement and racial equity are high-heat; the 8% Black population decline anchors the argument from both directions.

Recommended message: Honest framing: 'The plan documents the harm but is silent on the remedy. I'll defend Restorative Housing as executive responsibility — including in court.' (Matches her lit.)

Sources: p.272; Appendix A · City reparations program page

Protect immigrant neighbors

What the plan says: The plan's welcoming/inclusive vision (p. 48) and health/wraparound goals (p. 49) support it, but there is no specific city-property/employee protocol in the adopted text — that's an executive action, not a plan item.

Public sentiment: Not a major Envision flashpoint; low interference with plan fights. National climate makes it salient on its own.

Recommended message: Run it as an executive-order-style Day 1 commitment; no plan amendment needed.

Sources: pp.48–49 · City Welcoming City resources

Keep climate commitments moving (Healthy Buildings, CARP)

What the plan says: Strong plan backing: H 5.1 zero-emissions building codes, H 5.2 all-electric incentives (p. 132); ENV goals include mandatory composting and Lakefront Protection Ordinance commitments (p. 106); GA 11.4 eliminates downtown parking minimums (p. 96) — against engagement demands for MORE parking (p. 25).

Public sentiment: Parking is a medium-heat flashpoint with a real gap between plan text and what residents asked for.

Recommended message: Lead with efficiency = affordability (her lit's framing). On parking, don't volunteer GA 11.4; if pressed: corridor-by-corridor in the code phase.

Sources: H 5.1–5.2 p.132; p.106; GA 11.4 p.96

Research notes

  • CRITICAL CORRECTION FROM PRIOR SESSION: October 19, 2026 is NOT a primary and NOT the filing deadline. It is the FIRST day of the candidate filing period (127 days before the Feb 23, 2027 Consolidated Primary). The filing DEADLINE is Monday, October 26, 2026 (120 days before), per 10 ILCS 5/10-6(4) for nonpartisan municipal offices under Articles 4 and 5 of the Municipal Code. The identical Oct 19-26, 2026 window has been published officially by the Chicago Board of Elections for Chicago's Feb 23, 2027 municipal election, and Evanston's previous cycle used the identical day-counts (filed Oct 21-28, 2024 for the 2025 elections).
  • WHERE TO FILE: Evanston mayoral candidates file nominating papers with the Evanston CITY CLERK (local election official), not the Cook County Clerk and not ISBE. The Cook County Clerk administers the election itself (ballots, early voting, VBM, canvass) for suburban Cook County. The committee's campaign finance reports (D-2 quarterlies, A-1s) are filed electronically with the Illinois State Board of Elections.
  • PRIMARY IS CONTINGENT: Evanston city offices are nonpartisan. A February primary for mayor happens only if MORE THAN 4 mayoral candidates are on the ballot after objections; the top 2 finishers advance to April 6. In 2025 no Evanston race triggered a primary; in 2017 a 5-candidate mayoral field did trigger the Feb 28, 2017 primary. Plan compliance for the primary as if it will happen; you will know by the Dec 17, 2026 certification date at the latest.
  • ONE FILING, TWO POSSIBLE ELECTIONS: The October 2026 filing covers both the (contingent) Feb 23 primary and the Apr 6 election. Evanston city-office candidates do NOT file again in December 2026; the Nov/Dec 2026 windows that appear in ISBE materials (e.g., 141-134 days before the consolidated election) apply to school boards, park districts, and partisan/caucus municipalities - not to Evanston's nonpartisan city offices.
  • PETITION SIGNATURE COUNT: signature minimums/maximums for Evanston mayor (a percentage-based formula) are set by the Evanston City Clerk's candidate packet for 2027 - obtain the official packet from the City Clerk before circulating on July 28, 2026. Petition sheets must be signed on or after July 28, 2026 to be valid.
  • A-1 RULE (verified current, 10 ILCS 5/9-10): every contribution of $1,000 or more from a single source (including aggregated contributions from the same source in the reporting period, and loans, and candidate self-funding) must be reported on Schedule A-1 within 5 BUSINESS DAYS of receipt year-round; the deadline tightens to 2 BUSINESS DAYS when the contribution is received 30 or fewer days before an election in which the committee participates (Jan 24 - Feb 23, 2027 if an Evanston primary is held; Mar 7 - Apr 6, 2027 for the Consolidated Election). Late A-1s carry a civil penalty of up to 50% of the contribution. Illinois abolished separate pre-election D-2 reports; A-1s serve that function.
  • QUARTERLY D-2 deadlines are fixed by statute at the 15th of the month after each quarter: Oct 15, 2026; Jan 15, 2027; Apr 15, 2027; Jul 15, 2027 - all fall on business days in this cycle, so no weekend/holiday extensions apply.
  • STATUS LABELS: 'official' = the specific 2027 date appears in an official publication (ISBE Schedule of Elections; statute fixes the calendar date directly). 'derived' = computed from the controlling statute's day-count (statute cited in source) because the ISBE 2027 Election and Campaign Finance Calendar PDF, though published and listed on elections.il.gov's Running for Office page, could not be machine-read in this session. Every derived date should be reconciled against the ISBE 2027 calendar and the Cook County Clerk's 2027 calendar when convenient; the day-count rules were verified against current statute text and match the 2025-cycle official calendars exactly.
  • CONFLICT NOTED (minor): some older references say VBM ballots go out '30 days' before an election; the Chicago Board of Elections' official 2024 calendar and current ISBE-linked voter guidance use the 40th day (same day early voting begins) as the first VBM mailing day. This affects voters, not candidate compliance. Treat Jan 14, 2027 / Feb 25, 2027 as the practical first-mailing dates and confirm with the Cook County Clerk.
